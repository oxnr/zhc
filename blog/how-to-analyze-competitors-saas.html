How to Analyze Competitors: A Step-by-Step Guide for SaaS Founders

A practical walkthrough of competitive analysis methodology. No theory, no fluff. Just the steps that produce useful intelligence.

Before you start: define what you're looking for

Competitive analysis without a goal is just browsing. Before you open a single competitor's website, write down what decisions you're trying to make:

  • "We're about to set pricing for our new tier. What do competitors charge?"
  • "We lost three deals to CompetitorX last quarter. What are they doing that we're not?"
  • "We're preparing a Series A deck and need a clear competitive positioning slide."
  • "We're planning next quarter's roadmap and need to know which features are table stakes."

The goal shapes the analysis. A pricing-focused analysis is very different from a feature comparison. Trying to do everything at once is how competitive analysis projects die.

Step 1: Identify your real competitors

Most founders can name 2-3 competitors off the top of their head. That list is usually incomplete and sometimes wrong.

How to build a complete competitor list:

  1. Search your category keywords. Google what your customers would search. "project management tool," "invoice software," "CRM for startups." The first page results are your SEO competitors.
  2. Check review sites. G2 and Capterra have category pages. Find your category and see who else is listed. Sort by number of reviews to identify market leaders.
  3. Ask your sales team. "Who do we keep running into in deals?" The competitors your prospects mention are the ones that matter most.
  4. Search "alternative to [your product]." These pages list every substitute a prospect might consider.
  5. Check Product Hunt and Hacker News. Search for your category. New entrants show up here before they appear on review sites.

You'll end up with 5-15 names. Narrow to 3-5 for deep analysis. Pick the ones you actually lose deals to, plus any that are growing fast.

Step 2: Analyze their websites

A competitor's website is their public strategy document. They're telling you exactly how they position themselves, who they target, and what they think matters.

What to capture from each competitor's site:

  • Homepage hero: What's the headline? What problem do they lead with? What's the primary CTA?
  • Navigation structure: What sections do they prioritize? "Enterprise" in the nav means they're going upmarket. "Developers" means they're API-first.
  • Social proof: Who are their customers? What logos do they display? The customer list reveals their target market better than any positioning statement.
  • About page: Team size, funding, mission statement. A 200-person company with $50M in funding plays differently than a 10-person bootstrap.
  • Careers page: What roles are they hiring for? A burst of sales hiring signals go-to-market push. ML engineers signal AI investment. These are leading indicators of their strategy.

Step 3: Reverse-engineer their pricing

Pricing is the most important competitive data point. It tells you how a company values its product, who they think their customer is, and where they're positioned in the market.

What to capture:

  • Number of tiers and names
  • Price per tier (monthly and annual)
  • What's included in each tier (feature gates)
  • Free tier limits
  • Per-seat vs. flat pricing model
  • Any startup/discount programs

What to analyze:

  • Price anchoring: What's the "most popular" tier? That's where they make most revenue. Price yours relative to it.
  • Feature gates: Which features are free vs. paid? This reveals what they consider commoditized vs. valuable. If SSO is free at Competitor A but paid at Competitor B, that's a positioning difference.
  • Unit economics: Calculate cost per user at different team sizes (10, 50, 100 users). Pricing that looks similar for small teams can diverge dramatically at scale.

When we analyzed Linear vs. Shortcut vs. Plane, the pricing analysis revealed that Linear is the most expensive at every tier. For a 50-person team, Plane's Pro plan saves $6,000/year vs. Linear's Business plan. That data point alone changes how you position against them.

Step 4: Build a feature matrix

Feature comparison is the most time-consuming part of competitive analysis. But it's also the most actionable.

How to do it efficiently:

  1. List the 15-20 features that matter most in your category
  2. Check each competitor's features page, documentation, and changelog
  3. Mark each cell: Yes, No, Partial, or which tier it requires
  4. Highlight your advantages in green and gaps in red

The output you want: A clear picture of where you lead, where you trail, and where everyone is at parity. Parity features are table stakes. Gaps are vulnerabilities. Advantages are your sales ammunition.

Don't try to be comprehensive. Focus on the features that come up in sales conversations and customer requests. If nobody has ever asked you for a Gantt chart, don't waste time comparing Gantt chart implementations.

Step 5: Read their reviews

Customer reviews are the most underused source of competitive intelligence. Your competitors' customers will tell you things their marketing never will.

Where to read reviews:

  • G2 (most comprehensive for B2B SaaS)
  • Capterra
  • TrustRadius
  • Reddit (search "competitor name" + "review" or "experience")
  • Twitter/X (search for complaints and praise)

What to look for:

  • Common complaints: If 30% of reviews mention slow customer support, that's a real weakness you can exploit in sales conversations.
  • Praise patterns: What do users love? If everyone praises their onboarding, study it. Either match it or differentiate away from it.
  • Switching reasons: "We switched from X because..." is the most valuable sentence in any review. It tells you exactly what drives churn.
  • Missing features: "I wish it had..." tells you what their roadmap should include. If they haven't built it, maybe you should.

Start with the 1-star and 2-star reviews. They're where the real intelligence lives.

Step 6: Check their SEO and content

A competitor's content strategy reveals what keywords they're betting on and what audience they're building.

Quick SEO analysis (no paid tools needed):

  • Google their brand name: What shows up? Reviews, comparisons, alternatives pages? This is what your shared prospects see.
  • Read their blog: What topics do they write about? How often do they publish? The blog reveals their content strategy and target audience.
  • Check their backlinks: Use a free tool like Ahrefs Webmaster or Ubersuggest to see who links to them. Guest posts, directories, and PR tell you about their distribution strategy.
  • Search "your keyword" + their brand: Are they ranking for the terms you want? If they're on page 1 for "best project management tool" and you're not, that's a content gap to close.

Step 7: Turn research into action items

Research without action is just reading. The final step is the one that matters: what are you going to do differently?

After completing steps 1-6, write down your action items in this format:

  • This week: 1-2 things you can do immediately (update pricing page, add a comparison section, brief sales on a competitor weakness)
  • This month: 2-3 changes that need some work (adjust pricing tiers, prioritize a feature gap, create a competitive battlecard)
  • This quarter: 1-2 strategic moves (launch a startup program, build a missing feature, reposition messaging)

Each action item should be specific enough that someone could execute it without asking you for clarification.

"Launch a startup program to counter Shortcut's 12-month free offer" is an action item. "Consider our competitive positioning" is not.

How often to repeat this

Markets move. Competitors ship features, change pricing, and pivot positioning. A competitive analysis has a shelf life of about 3-6 months before it needs refreshing.

Set a calendar reminder. Or, if you'd rather not spend another 10 hours on manual research, let AI do the refresh.
